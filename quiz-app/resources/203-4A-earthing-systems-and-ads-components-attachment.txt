Lesson: 203.4A - Earthing Systems and ADS Components
Unit: 203
Audience: Level 2 electrical learner

Use the attachment one section at a time.

=== SECTION: Intro ===
1. Identify the primary types of earthing system arrangements.
2. Describe the function of specific components within an ADS system.
3. Classify components according to their role in achieving disconnection.
Open the lesson naturally and explain that the session will run in short chunks followed by questions.

=== SECTION: Teach/Check 1 ===
Core idea
The main difference between TT, TN-S, and TN-C-S is how the installation gets its earth path back to the source.

Must teach
- TN-S has separate neutral and earth conductors from the source.
- TN-C-S combines neutral and earth in the supply, then separates them at the intake position.
- TT does not rely on a supplier earth conductor and instead uses a local earth electrode.
- The type of earthing arrangement affects the fault path and the likely loop impedance.

Recognition cues
- TN-S: separate earth provided by the supplier, often via cable sheath.
- TN-C-S: PEN conductor in the supply, split at the intake.
- TT: local earth rod or electrode at the installation.

Why it matters
You must identify the earthing arrangement correctly before testing, fault-finding, or judging whether Automatic Disconnection of Supply will operate properly.

Common misconception
- A TT system is not automatically safer just because it has its own earth rod. The earth path can have higher impedance and often requires an RCD to achieve ADS safely.

Question targets for this chunk
1. Name the three earthing system types being compared.
2. Which arrangement uses a local earth electrode?
3. In which arrangement are neutral and earth combined in the supply and separated at the intake?
4. Deeper question: Why does the earthing arrangement matter when deciding whether fault protection will operate correctly?

=== SECTION: Teach/Check 2 ===
Core idea
ADS works when the fault current has a complete path back to the source and the protective device disconnects the supply quickly enough.

Must teach
- The CPC provides the return path from exposed conductive parts to the main earthing terminal.
- The protective device may be an overcurrent device or an RCD depending on the arrangement and fault conditions.
- Zs must be low enough, or the protective device may not disconnect quickly enough.
- Low Zs leads to higher fault current and faster disconnection.

Practical anchor
- Use a toaster or metal appliance casing to explain what happens when a live conductor touches metalwork.

Why it matters
If the CPC is missing, damaged, or poorly connected, or if the loop impedance is too high, a fault may leave metalwork live.

Common misconceptions
- The CPC does not normally carry current all the time; it carries current during a fault.
- Any fuse or breaker will not do. The protective device must suit the circuit and the fault conditions.

Question targets for this chunk
1. What is the role of the CPC?
2. What must be true about Zs for ADS to work properly?
3. What is the job of the protective device during a fault?
4. Deeper question: How do the CPC, protective device, and Zs work together to make a fault safe?

=== SECTION: Guided practice 1 ===
Scenario
- You are inspecting an installation that uses a local earth electrode and no supplier-provided earth conductor.

Guided prompts
1. Which earthing arrangement does that describe?
2. What does that tell you about the fault path compared with a metallic supplier earth?
3. Why might a more sensitive protective device such as an RCD be needed?

Expected points
- TT
- higher impedance through the electrode or ground path
- an RCD may be needed because the fault current may not be high enough for overcurrent protection alone

=== SECTION: Practice 1 ===
Independent checks
1. Why does a TT system often need a more sensitive protective device such as an RCD?
2. Which arrangement uses a PEN conductor?
3. In a TN-S system, how many separate supply conductors are provided for line, neutral, and earth?
4. What is the dedicated conductor that carries fault current from exposed metalwork back to the earthing system?

=== SECTION: Integrative 1 ===
Prompt
Explain how the physical earthing arrangement changes the fault path and how that affects the operation of ADS.

Key points
- the arrangement determines the available earth return path
- the CPC gives the internal fault-current path from exposed metalwork
- the protective device disconnects when fault current is high enough
- low enough Zs is needed for fast disconnection
