Top 5 Evidence-Based Approaches (what to build)
1. Retrieval Practice with Corrective Feedback

Strongest evidence overall

Learners must generate answers from memory, not recognize them.

Immediate or slightly delayed feedback corrects errors.

Works across vocational, GCSE, and A-level, especially in science/technical subjects.

Implication for AI apps:
AI should prompt recall first, then diagnose errors, then explain—never explain first.

2. Worked Examples → Faded Guidance

Critical for novices

Seeing correct, annotated solutions builds schemas.

Gradual removal of steps forces independent reasoning.

Especially strong for vocational learners and GCSE.

Implication:
AI alternates between:

“Here’s how it works”

“Now you do one part”

“Now do it all”

3. Socratic Questioning Targeting Misconceptions

Key for conceptual understanding

Asking why, what would happen if, what’s wrong here exposes faulty mental models.

Most effective when tied to known misconceptions, not generic “why?” questions.

Implication:
AI needs a misconception map, not free-form chat.

4. Spacing + Interleaving

Primary driver of long-term retention

Revisiting concepts days/weeks later beats massed practice.

Mixing related topics improves transfer and exam performance.

Implication:
AI schedules return questions, not just linear lessons.

5. Mastery Gating (Evidence-Based Unlocking)

Prevents illusion of competence

Learners advance only after demonstrating understanding via retrieval.

Completion ≠ mastery.

Implication:
Unlocks triggered by correct explanations or successful transfer, not time spent.

What the Evidence Is Strongest For

Conceptual understanding: very strong

Long-term retention: very strong

Exam performance: strong but mediated by alignment with assessment format

Procedural accuracy: strong when paired with step-checking and error diagnosis

What’s Less Certain

Claims about AI “personalization” improving learning

Purely conversational tutoring without structured tasks

Engagement metrics predicting learning outcomes

Top Pitfalls (what NOT to build)

Passive content consumption (reading/watching without retrieval)

Too much multiple choice (recognition ≠ understanding)

Ungated progression (“Next” buttons everywhere)

Immediate answer-giving by AI (kills learning effort)

Shallow gamification (points without thinking)

Blocked practice mistaken for mastery

Over-scaffolding that never fades

“Personalized” pacing without cognitive rationale

AI dependency (learner stops retrieving independently)

Completion metrics used as success indicators

Each of these reliably produces confidence without competence.

Group-Specific Summary
Vocational / Technical (Level 2–3)

Benefit most from worked examples, procedural checks, and error diagnosis

AI should emphasize why steps exist, not just how

GCSE

Strong gains from retrieval + feedback, explicit misconception correction

Socratic questioning must be structured and concrete

A-Level

Best supported by interleaving, transfer problems, and explanatory writing

AI should push beyond recall into causal and relational reasoning

The Winning AI App Loop (in plain English)

Present a concept briefly

Force recall or explanation

Detect the error or gap

Ask a targeted Socratic question

Provide explanation only if needed

Re-test

Schedule future retrieval

Unlock next concept only after mastery

If your app does this reliably, the evidence says it will outperform most commercial “learning” apps—regardless of how flashy they are.
